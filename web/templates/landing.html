What is Nephrotic Syndrome?

Nephrotic syndrome (NS) is a kidney disorder characterized by a group of clinical features including proteinuria (excessive protein in urine, typically >3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminemia (low blood albumin levels), edema (swelling), and hyperlipidemia (elevated blood lipids)[10]. This condition results from damage to the glomeruli‚Äîthe tiny filtering units in the kidneys that prevent protein loss into urine.

The underlying pathophysiology involves disruption of the glomerular filtration barrier, particularly affecting podocytes (specialized epithelial cells) and the slit diaphragm proteins that maintain the selective permeability of the glomerular basement membrane[9]. When this barrier is compromised, proteins that should remain in the bloodstream leak into the urine, leading to the cascade of symptoms characteristic of nephrotic syndrome.

Classification of Nephrotic Syndrome

SSNS

Steroid-Sensitive Nephrotic Syndrome

Responds well to corticosteroid therapy, typically showing remission within 4-8 weeks of treatment[5]. Accounts for approximately 80-90% of childhood nephrotic syndrome cases. Generally has a favorable prognosis with appropriate treatment.

SRNS

Steroid-Resistant Nephrotic Syndrome

Does not respond to standard corticosteroid therapy. Associated with genetic mutations in podocyte genes (NPHS1, NPHS2, WT1, LAMB2, etc.)[6]. Represents 10-20% of childhood cases but carries higher risk of progression to end-stage renal disease (ESRD)[5].

CNS

Congenital Nephrotic Syndrome

Manifests within the first 3 months of life, often in utero. Frequently caused by mutations in NPHS1 (nephrin) or NPHS2 (podocin) genes[3]. Particularly prevalent in Finnish population (Finnish-type CNS). Requires early intervention and often progresses to kidney failure.

Adult NS

Adult-Onset Nephrotic Syndrome

Develops in adulthood with diverse etiologies including focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), membranous nephropathy, minimal change disease, and secondary causes (diabetes, lupus, infections)[2]. May have genetic or acquired causes. Treatment approach varies based on underlying pathology.

üî¨ Genetic Basis

Over 60 genes have been identified as causative or risk factors for nephrotic syndrome[9]. These genes primarily encode proteins critical for podocyte structure and function, including slit diaphragm proteins (NPHS1, NPHS2, CD2AP), actin cytoskeleton regulators (ACTN4, INF2, MYO1E), and nuclear pore complex components (NUP93, NUP107, NUP205). Mutations in these genes can lead to monogenic forms of NS, while common variants contribute to polygenic risk[6].

Global Disease Burden & Prevalence

The incidence and prevalence of nephrotic syndrome vary significantly across different ethnic populations and age groups, reflecting both genetic and environmental factors[1][4]. Understanding these patterns is crucial for targeted screening programs and resource allocation in healthcare systems worldwide.

üåè Key Geographic Observations

Nephrotic syndrome shows remarkable geographic and ethnic disparities. Asian populations, particularly South Asians, experience 2-5 times higher incidence rates compared to European and North American populations[1]. These disparities reflect complex interactions between genetic susceptibility, environmental factors, and access to healthcare[4]. Understanding these patterns is crucial for targeted screening programs and resource allocation in healthcare systems worldwide.

Molecular Pathways & Protein Interactions

Why Does Nephrotic Syndrome Happen?

üî¨

Immune Dysregulation

Primarily in Steroid-Sensitive Nephrotic Syndrome

  • Abnormal immune response affecting kidney filtration[10]
  • Often triggered by infections or environmental factors
  • T-cell dysfunction leads to podocyte damage[9]
  • Responsive to immunosuppressive treatments[5]
üß¨

Genetic Mutations

Primarily in Steroid-Resistant Nephrotic Syndrome

  • Mutations in podocyte structural genes[6]
  • Over 60 causative genes identified[9]
  • Directly affects kidney filtering barrier[3]
  • Often resistant to standard immunotherapy[5]
